# Source Notes: Relativistic Kinematics

These notes are batch-specific extraction context, not canonical graph facts.

## Intended Reading of the Topic

For this batch, `relativistic kinematics` should be understood in the standard special-relativity sense:

- how events and measurements are related across inertial frames,
- how invariant quantities are defined and used,
- and how the standard kinematic relations are applied in routine calculations.

This batch is intended to support paper, lecture-note, and textbook reading in the QFT/HEP-th foundation setting, not a derivation-first reconstruction of the Lorentz group or a full treatment of relativistic dynamics.

## What the Batch Is Trying to Capture

This batch should identify the smallest defensible prerequisite slice needed to:

- recognize the core vocabulary of relativistic kinematics,
- follow the standard transformations between inertial frames,
- and use the common formula families in ordinary worked examples.

The batch is not trying to capture:

- all of special relativity,
- relativistic dynamics or forces,
- curved spacetime,
- or a complete axiomatization of spacetime structure.

## Likely High-Value Node Candidates

The most likely node candidates are:

- inertial frame
- event
- spacetime coordinates
- Lorentz transformation
- invariant interval
- proper time
- time dilation
- length contraction
- relativity of simultaneity
- velocity addition
- rapidity
- four-velocity
- four-momentum when it is used as a kinematic quantity rather than a dynamics topic

## Likely Edge Patterns

High-value dependency patterns probably include:

- event and coordinate concepts into Lorentz transformations
- Lorentz transformations into invariant interval and proper time
- Lorentz transformations into time dilation, length contraction, and simultaneity
- velocity addition and rapidity as use-level tools for frame changes
- four-vector concepts only when they are introduced as compact kinematic representations

## Source Guidance

Prioritize introductory special-relativity textbook sections, lecture notes, and review passages that explicitly define the kinematic objects and show how they are used.

Prefer a small source set with stable terminology over broad secondary references or highly formal derivations.

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- broad special-relativity or mechanics umbrellas,
- pushing the batch into acceleration, force, or worldline dynamics unless directly needed,
- importing general relativity or field theory,
- and treating a derivation from first principles as mandatory for every node.

## Evidence Guidance

Use evidence that shows:

- how authors define the kinematic objects,
- which relations are treated as standard tools,
- where a relation is used to interpret measurements across inertial frames,
- and when a concept is presented as background rather than the main claim.

If a candidate feels derivation-heavy, flag it for later expansion instead of forcing it into the first-wave scaffold.
